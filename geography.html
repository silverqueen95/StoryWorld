The geography of your world will have a significant impact on the story. Long journeys through perilous environments are a staple of the fantasy genre. Mixing fantasy elements with real geographic features will help your world feel grounded and unique.

Creating the map of your world is perhaps one of the most challenging parts of worldbuilding, especially if you do not have the artistic skills to draw your own. A common method is the "coffee stain" method. Simply take some coffee and randomly drip it on a piece of paper. You can also shape the map around the type of story you want to tell. Is there long journey through a variety of environments, or is the landscape consistently the same throughout the world? Are there multiple continents with different climates? These are important elements to consider when creating a map.

When creating your map, considering the geographic features of the country, town, or region is a crucial step. Is the evironment full of rolling hills or is it a barren desert? If the land is barren, how do the residents find food and water? It may seem silly to worry about realism in a fantasy story, but keeping your universe grounded in some real world logic will help your audience feel more immeresed in the story. Consider how these different landscapes affects the cultures of the peoples who live there. In real life, regions with abdundant resources tend to produce less internal conflict, while harsh, infertile lands Features fierce competition for resources. The geographic features may also present obstacles for the characters. A great example of is comes from the Lord of the Rings books. The Fellowship must travel through the snowy mountains which the villian, Sarumon, takes advantage of by using magic to cause an avalanche. This is a great way to add conflict to your story.
